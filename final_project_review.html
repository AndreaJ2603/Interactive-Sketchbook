Final Project Review

It is that the human mind naturally gravitates toward the path of least effort, likely by design. I think life's complexity has trained our brains to simply take shortcuts.
With technology being still new to the world, many people assume devices will be difficult to use—even when instructions are clear—and still hesitate or second-guess them.

The minute I encounter a bump or glitch in an interaction at least for myself, it will destroy the whole experience ahead with that device.
From that moment on all I will look up is for the flaws on that system until I am proved right so I can move to something better!!

I chose a simple approach for my project to make it easy to use. However, simplicity can sometimes feel complex, because our minds no longer readily accept
it due to the constant stimulation and experiences we encounter every day.

I think the affordance in my microproject is the interaction of the user with the mouse.These affordances are signified by the user's decisions to click at a
certain time to score points. The signifier is to click any key on the keyboard and anywhere on the screen to play the game.

I also believe that displaying the countdown timer and score on the screen not only provides feedback but also signifies to the user the time limit they will have to
interact with the system.

If a user encountered my system for the first time they would know what to do because most users have had experience with games. It also states on the screen to click to start.
I do not think that there was any hidden affordance or any uncertainty because once the user starts the interaction the discoverability on the system is straightforward.

It is a simple one step forward action. When the game ends, it will tell the user to click again to start over. However, it ultimately depends on the user, particularly their age group.
The product I designed targets a specific group, and testing it with that audience would better reveal its flaws and any hidden affordances.

The system provides visual feedback by showing time and score which shows to the user their immediate progress and how much time they would have to achieve a successful
interaction with the sytem.

The user also gets feedback on their performance at the end of the game. If sufficient points are collected, a flower is presented at the end of the interaction.

The system provides mapping because it moves continuously from one side (left) to the other side (right). These movements are autonomous and the user cannot stop it.
There is no going back and there is only one direction to go and this the only way to operate the system which implies a constraint as well.

There is also a certain level of frustration added that imposes a constraint on the system. The system is set up for the shapes to flash at varying speeds.
The user may assume that the same speed will remain constant so they can predict the specific time click and score. I think this mismatch can lead to frustration which was also reflected in feedback I received about this specific aspect of the game’s functionality.

Yes, there is confusion in the system and it was not intentional. The system provides an intuitive idea that the user must click on the moving shapes on the screen to score points.
Even though instruction clearly says click anywhere it might lead to confusion for the user. I think this is one of the biggest design failures. If Norman evaluated my project I think he would say the same as well.

In design, providing clear guidance can be harder than building the product itself. As human behavior evolves, design might become continously adaptive to meet changing user expectations.
I think that a good design feels effortless: when users can move forward without confusion or doubt, the experience is successful. I believe that, in most cases, we should prioritize the user's experience when redesigning a product or improving its functionality. However, simplicity is not always perceived as intended,
as users may impose their own sense of complexity onto a design, even when none actually exists.

My biggest takeaway from the text is that complexity in design will continue to increase. As a designer, I aim to keep my work simple.
